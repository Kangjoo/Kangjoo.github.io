Innovations in Functional Neuroimaging Analysis

Understanding how brain functions are mapped within the brain is a key question in neuroscience. Task-based functional magnetic resonance imaging (fMRI) is widely used to localize functions associated with tasks within the brain. Traditional approaches based on statistical parametric mapping (SPM) employ a mass-univariate general linear model for task fMRI, which assumes a hypothesis-driven model that is fixed for every voxel and for every subject. Not accounting for variations in task-evoked fMRI response across voxels and people may impact the power and reliability of fMRI findings. We addressed this limitation by developing a novel approach to estimate data-driven, individual-specific, and voxel-specific models for SPM (Lee K, 2011). This was one of the pioneering works leveraging sparse dictionary learning, an unsupervised machine learning approach, for task fMRI. Subsequently, we extended our single-subject level analysis to group-level analysis, in addition to accommodating the analysis of resting-state fMRI (Lee YB, 2016). We released an open-source software tool, Sparse SPM, which incorporates all these functionalities. Next, I focused on understanding the functional organization of the brain using resting-state fMRI. Brain networks are often identified by measuring temporal correlation of fMRI signals in distinct brain regions. Hub regions participating in between-network connectivity are important for long-range communications. Traditional approaches to identify hubs using pairwise Pearson’s correlation are biased by the multicollinearity of functional signals in the brain. Furthermore, fMRI signals observed in a voxel is a hemodynamics consequence of complex processes involving hundreds of thousands of neurons, suggesting involvement of a voxel in multiple functional networks. In Lee K (2016), we addressed these challenges by employing the sparse general linear model (Lee K, 2011). Our proposed method, a sparsity-based analysis of reliable k-hubness (SPARK) demonstrated a unique ability to define hubs by measuring k-hubness, the number (k) of functional networks overlapping in each voxel. Critically, we assessed the statistical reproducibility of hub analysis at the single-subject level, identifying hubs with high reproducibility within each subject, which in turn can reveal a reproducible combination of overlapping networks in these hubs. We released an open-source software tool for SPARK. Furthermore, we developed a method for automatic classification and removal of physiological noise in fMRI to improve the hub estimation (Lee K, 2019).

Development of Neural Markers of Epilepsy, Sleep and Cognition

Epilepsy is primarily characterized by recurrent epileptic seizures. In drug-resistant patients with mesial temporal lobe epilepsy (mTLE), up to 30% of patients do not achieve seizure freedom even after surgical resection of the seizure-generating brain regions. In Lee K (2018) and Dansereau CL (2014), we identified abnormal patterns of network reorganizations in focal epilepsy using EEG/fMRI, even beyond the regions associated with epileptic seizure generation. Leveraging the unique ability of SPARK to measure k-hubness and identify a reproducible combination of networks overlapping in each hub, our study demonstrated pathological disruptions of normal hubs in the mesial temporal lobe and within the default mode network (Lee K, 2018). We identified patient-specific patterns of hub connectivity and characterized distinct patterns of hub emergence and lateralization of hippocampal networks in right versus left mTLE (Lee K, 2018). We also investigated the patterns of hub connectivity in non-rapid eye movement (NREM) sleep to understand its role in cognitive function. Acquiring and analyzing simultaneous EEG/fMRI data with a three-day protocol including a 24-hour total sleep deprivation, we found altered functional integration of brain activity during NREM sleep as a marker of impaired cognitive performance following sleep deprivation (Cross N, 2021). Then, we developed a voxel-level index of hierarchical network segregation using k-hubness and found distinct patterns of brain network segregation during NREM 2 and NREM 3 sleep stages, across spatial scales and brain regions (Lee K and Wang Y, submitted). Our results suggested N3 sleep-specific roles of network reorganization and that different characteristics of brain network reorganizations across brain regions may occur at different spatial resolutions. In addition, I collaborated on the development of advanced EEG technology for studying neural activity in sleep (Uji M, under revision).

Metabolic and Physiological Signatures of Human Brain Networks

People who undergo resting-state fMRI scans experience spontaneous fluctuations of arousal level. We found that brain network hubs are reorganized across arousal levels during resting state, using changes in pupil area measured by simultaneous pupillometry recordings as a behavioral index of arousal (Lee K, 2022). By stratifying resting-state fMRI data into high versus low arousal states and identifying reproducible brain network hubs at each arousal state using SPARK (Lee K, 2016), we showed a brain-wide decrease in network integration with reduced arousal, contributing to inter-subject variability. Furthermore, we recently submitted a manuscript from a 6-year multi-lab collaboration lead by Razavipour SF (Submitted), which studied the oxygen and glucose metabolic signatures of brain network hubs identified using SPARK for resting-state fMRI. In this study, we found a non-linear relationship between k-hubness and energy metabolism at the voxel level that suggests high energy efficiency in brain network hubs, quantifying the cerebral metabolic rates of glucose (CMRGlu) and oxygen (CMRO2) using positron emission tomography (PET) and calibrated fMRI. My earlier work involved developing methods to quantify CMRO2 using simultaneous near-infrared spectroscopy and fMRI (Tak S, 2010) and optimizing of detector modules of small animal PET (Chung YH, 2009).

Reproducible Mental Health Research with Open Science and Artificial Intelligence

Reproducibility plays a key role when identifying and quantifying the complex relationship between neural activity and human behavior. Considering that the sample size is a critical factor determining the reproducibility of neuroimaging research, it is helpful to know how to use open-source datasets and repositories. These datasets provide multi-site, multi-modal neuroimaging data acquired from many populations. We published an article that offers practical guidelines for working with large, open-source neuroimaging datasets from an end-user’s perspectives (Horien C, 2020a), highlighting important steps and considerations in the analysis of large open-source data and in neuroimaging-based predictive modelling. Besides, we proposed a safe and effective mock scan protocol to obtain low-motion fMRI data from pediatric participants undergoing a one-hour scan (Horien C, 2020b). I recently collaborated on a large study investigating the front-end application of artificial intelligence (AI) in neuroscience (Luo X, In Press). Serving as the 2023-24 chair of the Diversity and Inclusivity Committee of the Organization for Human Brain Mapping, I addressed the lack of diversity in and accessibility to publicly available large datasets, challenges in AI for brain research and neuroimaging-based AI models, suggesting potential solutions to overcome these barriers (Kam and Badhwar, 2024). In addition, I lead an international initiative in 2022-24 that aims to make neuroscience accessible to children worldwide and to nurture future neuroscientists from historically underrepresented backgrounds (Lee K and Borghesani V, 2024).

Developing Neural Markers of Psychopathology

Recently, I quantified moment-to-moment changes in brain co-activated patterns of resting-state fMRI in a healthy general population, identifying a low-dimensional set of state (varying within a person) and trait (varying across people)-related features of neural dynamics that are highly reproducible at the single subject level (Lee K, 2024). This is the first study that leverages the entire range of BOLD signals to demonstrate low-dimensional motifs of individual differences in neural features derived from brain co-activation states are associated to life functional behavioral outcomes including cognition, emotion regulation, substance use and psychiatric functions. Working with my colleagues at Yale Psychiatry, I have been studying neuro-symptom variations across the mood (Berkovitch L, under revision), psychosis (Lee K and Fonteneau C, 2024), and anxiety spectra (Ben-Zion Z and Lee K, 2024). Furthermore, we use pharmacological neuroimaging, which induces a model of psychosis with a glutamate receptor antagonist, and study alternations of specific brain functions (spatial tuning) and how that relates to cognitive deficits such as working memory (Rahmati M, submitted). Collectively, in this highly collaborative research environment, combined with my world-class mentorship team at Yale, I will receive strong clinical training to develop expertise in understanding symptom variations and measurements in psychopathology.
